when was the big bang theory theorized and who theorized it (and what country were they from?)

Claude

The Big Bang theory was proposed by Georges Lemaître, a Belgian physicist and Catholic priest.

He laid out the core idea in two steps:
- 1927 — he derived an expanding-universe model from Einstein's equations and proposed that the universe was growing.
- 1931 — he proposed that this expansion meant the universe began from a single, dense initial point, which he called the "primeval atom" (sometimes the "Cosmic Egg").

So he was from Belgium.

A couple of footnotes worth knowing: the actual name "Big Bang" came later, coined by the British astronomer Fred Hoyle in 1949 — somewhat dismissively, since Hoyle favored a competing steady-state model. And Edwin Hubble's 1929 observations of receding galaxies provided the key empirical backing that Lemaître's expansion idea predicted.

is nuclear power the most logical energy source for space? where does the cooling center go? do you need water? isn't space cold?

Claude

Let me take your last question first, because it's a great reversal.

"Isn't space cold?" Space isn't *cold* the way a freezer is. Cold usually means a substance around you carrying heat away — cold air, cold water. But space is a near-vacuum. There's almost no matter to carry your heat off, and a vacuum is one of the best *insulators* in existence — that's how a thermos works.

So a powered object in space can only shed heat one way: radiation, glowing it away as infrared. It can't conduct or convect to surroundings that aren't there. The upshot, which catches everyone off guard: the dominant danger for spacecraft is *overheating*, not freezing.

Cooling and water. Since radiation is the only exit, spacecraft carry radiators — big panels that glow waste heat into space. Water comes in not as the final heat sink but as a *delivery truck*: pumped coolant carries heat to the radiators, which radiate it away; the fluid loops back. The ISS uses water internally and ammonia externally. You don't consume it.

Is nuclear most logical? It depends where you are:
- Inner solar system: sunlight is strong, so solar panels usually win. Most satellites and the ISS run on solar.
- Deep space: sunlight weakens with distance, so nuclear takes over. Voyager, Cassini, the Mars rovers all run on radioisotope generators.
- Long darkness or high power: a Moon base's two-week night, a crewed outpost — nuclear's energy density beats solar-plus-batteries.

The catch: nuclear hands you a cooling bill. Most of a reactor's heat is waste that must be radiated away — so the more power you make, the bigger the radiator wings you grow.

Yes — and the difference is dramatic, because on a surface you sometimes get back the cooling tricks that vacuum took away. It comes down to: what's around you to carry heat off?

Mars is genuinely easier, thanks to its thin atmosphere. It's only 0.6% of Earth's pressure, but that's the difference between "nothing" and "something" — you get convection back. So Mars cools by a mix of radiation and convection, much closer to how we'd do it on Earth.

There's also a quiet bonus to any gravity: it gives you natural convection in your coolant loops, which doesn't happen in microgravity.

The spectrum is telling: Titan would be a cooling paradise (dense, frigid nitrogen atmosphere). Venus is the nightmare — plenty of atmosphere, but at +465°C it's hotter than any radiator, and you can't reject heat to something hotter than your source.

So yes — on Mars the atmosphere solves "somewhat" of the cooling for you; on the Moon you stay radiator-dependent but lean on shadowed cold traps. The rule that runs through it all: cooling gets easier the more cold matter you have around you to carry heat away.

What fraction do the giants eject? The majority. Of all the icy material originally scattered, only a few percent stayed in the Oort cloud; most was thrown clean out of the solar system. Jupiter is especially ruthless.

And yes — it's a "loop." It's the gravitational slingshot. A comet swings past Jupiter on a curved flyby; relative to the planet it leaves at the same speed, just redirected — but relative to the Sun it can gain a huge amount of speed by "stealing" some of Jupiter's orbital motion. Gain enough and it exceeds escape velocity and is flung out forever.
